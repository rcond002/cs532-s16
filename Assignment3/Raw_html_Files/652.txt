He proclaimed the professional kitchen "the last true refuge of the misfit," and while many would argue that still holds true nearly two decades later, even now there pervades an unfortunate double standard in which the sort of so-called dysfunction that often drives people toward cooking as a profession is still too heavily stigmatized to talk about.</p>\n<p dir="ltr"><span>One woman is on a quest to change that: Kat Kinsman, the food writer formerly of CNN and current editor-at-large of Tasting Table, has just launched a project she\xe2\x80\x99s calling <a href="http://chefswithissues.com" target="_blank">Chefs With Issues</a>, dedicated to <b>destigmatizing mental illness in the culinary industry</b> and helping those affected by it get the help they hard as hell on the psyche."</q></p>\n<p dir="ltr"><span>"</span><span>For a profession that\xe2\x80\x99s so much about sensual joy and physical satisfaction for the end consumer, it\xe2\x80\x99s hard as hell on the psyche," <a href="http://chefswithissues.com/2016/01/01/speaking-out/" target="_blank">Kinsman writes in an introductory blog entry</a> on the site. </span><span>Take a high-stress environment, add a heavy dose of notoriously long hours and a dash of drugs and alcohol, and you have a recipe for some serious mental health issues \xe2\x80\x94 whether they\'re pre-existing or brought on by the stress of the job.</span></p>\n<p dir="ltr"><span><span>And in the traditionally male-dominated culinary industry where </span><span>machismo still largely reigns supreme, any displays of so-called weaknesses in professional kitchens have a tendency to be exploited. </span>"</span><span>Seeking help for depression, anxiety, bipolar disorder, OCD, addiction, not only is it a healthcare\xe2\x80\x8a\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x8ait\xe2\x80\x99s stigmatized," Kinsman writes. <b>"We\xe2\x80\x99re stopping each other from seeking help, and that\xe2\x80\x99s got to change."</b></span></p>\n<p dir="ltr">Kinsman has written about her own depression and anxiety and spoken about it on television, even writing a book about her experiences (called <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/0062369687/ref=as_li_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=9325&amp;creativeASIN=0062369687&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;tag=hianxiety-20&amp;linkId=M7CTHOKS3P6R47BM" As she became more vocal about her own mental health, Kinsman says she found that more and more chefs felt comfortable enough to confide in her about their own struggles: "I started to realize, it\'s rampant in the industry," she tells Eater. "Eventually, the volume got to me and I just decided, it\'s time to talk about it."\xc2\xa0<span>She cites </span><span><i>Food &amp; Wine</i></span><span> editor-in-chief Dana Cowin and </span><span><i>Saveur</i></span><span><i> </i>founding editor/cookbook author Dorothy Kalins as "huge inspirations" for encouraging her to launch the project.</span></p>\n<p><q class="pullquote float-right">"People are afraid of being thought of as crazy. "</span>I realize not everybody can do that, and that\'s who I\xe2\x80\x99m fighting for," she tells Eater. "As more people who are thought of as established or successful or notable in their field can stand up and say \'Look, I deal with these issues,\' it\xe2\x80\x99s going to make it easier for everybody else."</p>\n<p dir="ltr"><span>The response to the Chefs With Issues mission has been measuredly robust so far: Less than 24 hours after the site\xe2\x80\x99s launch, Kinsman says she\xe2\x80\x99s already received around 100 responses to the </span><a href="http://chefswithissues.com/2016/01/02/food-worker-survey/" target="_blank">mental health survey</a> aimed at food professionals<span>, and scores of emails and DMs about the project. </span><span>"Reading through these survey responses, I\xe2\x80\x99m seeing that people are afraid of being thought of as crazy.
