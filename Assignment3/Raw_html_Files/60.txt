By combining the data from these surveys, we can explore the views of different groups of voters in detail.\xc2\xa0</p>\n<p>They show that one of the biggest challenges facing the pro-EU camp will be to enthuse the under 30s, who are mostly pro-membership but traditionally least likely to vote at all, while the \xe2\x80\x98leave\xe2\x80\x99 camp needs to maximise turnout among Brexit-inclined working-class voters. And both sides need to target Tory voters, who currently divide 55-45% for leaving the EU. This is a far more even division than among supporters of any other party.\xc2\xa0</p>\n<p>Other YouGov research has found that many EU-sceptic Conservative voters are ready to switch sides if David Cameron campaigns for his renegotiated deal. If he can also get Boris Johnson to back his position, then the shift could be big enough to deliver a decisive overall majority for staying in the EU. If Boris and the PM take different sides, then the referendum result is far less certain.\xc2\xa0</p>\n<p>It is no surprise that most of those who voted Ukip last May strongly oppose EU membership, or that most Labour, Liberal Democrat, Green and SNP voters disagree. However, as many as 28% of Ukip voters \xe2\x80\x93 more than one million people \xe2\x80\x93 say they would vote to stay in the EU; this supports the view that Ukip taps into a wider mood of discontent than that generated by debates about Europe.\xc2\xa0</p>\n<p>Views vary strongly by age. Voters under 30 support continued membership by almost two-to-one. Support falls to 58% among people in their thirties and 52% among those\xc2\xa0 in their forties. The over 50s favour Brexit: by 52-48% among those in their fifties\xc2\xa0 and by 56-44% among the over sixties.\xc2\xa0</p>\n<p>This looks like good news for the \xe2\x80\x98leave\xe2\x80\x99 campaign, as older electors are more likely to vote. On the other hand, university graduates, a high-turnout group, are strongly pro-EU while those with fewest educational qualifications, who are more likely to stay at home, favour Brexit.\xc2\xa0</p>\n<p>The biggest cleavage concerns newspaper readership. More than 70% of Guardian and Independent readers plan to vote for continued EU membership. At the other end of the scale, Express readers favour Brexit by 66-34%. However, these papers all have relatively small readerships; the two biggest-selling papers, the Daily Mail and Sun, have readers that also back Brexit, but more modestly; in both cases, four in ten readers plan to vote to stay in the EU.</p>\n<p>Neither paper has yet declared which side, if either, it will take in the referendum campaign. Should both campaign strongly for Brexit, day after day, One of the key factors that might determine the outcome of a close contest is whether their large minorities of pro-EU readers change sides and end up voting to leave. (On the other hand, if papers are that influential, the maybe the Mirror will be able to reduce the proportion of the 48% of its readers who are pro-Brexit.)</p>\n<p>If each paper ends up taking the side of a majority of its readers, then NewsUK\xe2\x80\x99s two papers will support opposing sides, with the Sun advocating Brexit, while the Times backs continued EU membership \xe2\x80\x93 the position of 62% of its readers.\xc2\xa0</p>\n<p>In one specific respect, the EU referendum looks like being very different from the Scottish independence vote in 2014. Then there was a bigger gender gap, with most men voting for separation, and most women opting to stay in the UK. On the EU, we find no gender gap at all. (as with many issues, far more women than men say \xe2\x80\x98don\xe2\x80\x99t know\xe2\x80\x99; but among those who take sides, there is no difference.)\xc2\xa0</p>\n<p>As in the last European referendum, in 1975, the figures for most regions are similar. But there are two big exceptions. Both London and Scotland clearly back continued EU membership; all other regions divide fairly evenly.</p>\n<p></p>\n<center><img
