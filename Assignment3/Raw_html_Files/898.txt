<strong>5.0%</strong>\n</li>\n<li>Average hourly earnings month-on-month:\xc2\xa0<strong>0.3%</strong>\n</li>\n<li>Average hourly earnings year-on-year:<strong> 2.2%</strong>\n</li>\n<li>Average data continue to suggest that the manufacturing sector remains hampered by weakening demand due to lower oil prices and the stronger dollar while the rest of the economy is resilient," writes a Nomura\'s Lewis Alexander.\xc2\xa0</span></p>\n<p><span><span>"As such, we expect manufacturing payrolls to recede by 5k jobs and the bulk of the job gains to come from the service providing sector."</span></span></p>\n<p><span><span>He outlined some of the recent ugly data with respect to manufacturing:</span></span></p>\n<p style="padding-left: 60px;">"Employment indicators from manufacturing surveys have been negative. <a href="http://www.businessinsider.com/ism-manufacturing-and-markit-pmi-2016-2">The national ISM manufacturing employment index</a> declined further in January to 45.9 from 48.0, implying employment contracted at a faster pace in January compared to December. The <a href="http://www.businessinsider.com/chicago-pmi-january-2016-1">Chicago PMI employment indicator</a> improved but remained below 50 at 48.9 in January. The number of employees index from the Empire State survey remained at depressed levels, while the comparable index from the <a href="http://www.businessinsider.com/philly-fed-manufacturing-survey-2016-1">Philly Fed survey</a> turned slightly negative after positive readings in the prior two months."</p>\n<h2>It wouldn\'t be a proper economics report without the weather</h2>\n<p>One other factor that could affect the report is the weather, argues Alexander. Specifically the fact that it it finally got cold in January after the unusually long-lasting warm weather in late 2015.</p>\n<p>"We find that the unseasonably warm weather in the\xc2\xa0past several months likely had a positive, albeit transitory, impact on economic activity in\xc2\xa0late 2015. Our analysis suggests that we should see some reversal in activity in weather-sensitive industries, as weather has returned closer to traditional norms. As such, we see\xc2\xa0downside risk to job growth in the construction sector and other weather sensitive-sectors," he wrote.</p>\n<p>And on a somewhat related note,\xc2\xa0since the\xc2\xa0holidays finally ended, some of the sectors picked up\xc2\xa0speed\xc2\xa0in November and December may have returned to a more modest pace of hiring.</p>\n<p>In particular, Goldman Sachs\' John Hatzius pointed to the possibility of a\xc2\xa0<span>decline in couriers and messengers\' employment (i.e. package delivery workers) of about 10-20k.</span></p>\n<h2>Seasonal quirk or something bigger?</h2>\n<p>But\xc2\xa0most importantly, economists will be looking to see\xc2\xa0whether\xc2\xa0the January jobs report is a sign\xc2\xa0Q4 slowdown is starting to show in the labor market or if this report might just be a seasonal quirk that will be revised up later.</p>\n<p>"<span>When the economy is at a turning point, the labor market always responds last, as employers turn to layoffs as a last resort during hard times.
